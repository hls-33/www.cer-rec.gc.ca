Canada’s Renewable Power Landscape 2017 – Energy Market Analysis

Canadian Overview

In 2016, Canada’s electricity generation was 66.0% renewable and was 80.6% non-emitting in operation. Since 2005, generation has trended away from coal and towards increased generation from natural gas and wind.

Generation Trends

Hydro is Canada’s largest source of electricity, averaging 59.6% of total generation from 2005 to 2016. Hydroelectricity is generated primarily in Yukon, B.C., Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, and Newfoundland and Labrador. Nuclear, currently located exclusively in Ontario and New Brunswick, supplied on average 14.7% of Canada’s electricity from 2005 to 2016, and was the second largest source of total generation. The share of natural gas generation increased from 6.8% to 9.6% between 2005 and 2016, while coal and oil generation declined, mainly due to new emissions-reduction plans, including Ontario’s coal phase-out.

Non-hydro renewable generation has grown since 2005, from 1.5% to 7.2% of total generation. Total wind generation grew the most during this period, increasing from just 0.2% to 4.7% of generation. Solar also saw growth, almost exclusively in Ontario, from zero in 2010 to 0.5% of Canadian electricity generation in 2016. Biomass grew from 1.3% to 2.0% of generation between 2005 and 2016.

Capacity Changes in 2016

Net Canadian natural gas, oil and diesel capacity decreased in 2016. This was primarily due to the retirement of the Burrard Thermal Generating Station, among other retirements.

Hydro, wind, biomass, and solar experienced an estimated combined growth of 2 261 MW. Together, renewables’ share of total Canadian capacity increased by 0.8% from 2015.
